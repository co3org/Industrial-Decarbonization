# Recent Advances in Carbon Capture Technologies for Steel and Cement Decarbonization

## Introduction

The steel and cement industries are among the most emissions-intensive sectors, collectively accounting for over 50% of industrial CO2 emissions globally. Steel production emits approximately 7-9% of total anthropogenic CO2, primarily from blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace (BF-BOF) processes, while cement contributes about 8%, largely due to clinker production and calcination. Carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies are pivotal for achieving net-zero goals in these hard-to-abate sectors, with recent breakthroughs focusing on cost reductions, energy efficiency, and scalable deployment. This document highlights key developments from 2024, emphasizing innovations that address technical and economic barriers.

## Key Technological Advances in Steel

### Low-Energy Carbon Capture Solutions
Capsol Technologies has developed a novel absorption-based carbon capture system using an aqueous potassium sarcosine (KSar) solvent, which requires up to 50% less energy than traditional amine-based systems. This technology targets flue gases from steel mills, capturing over 90% of CO2 at costs projected below $50 per ton. Pilot testing in 2024 demonstrated its integration with existing BF-BOF operations, potentially enabling retrofits without major infrastructure overhauls. [Capsol Technologies Overview](https://www.capsoltech.com/)

### Hydrogen-Enabled Direct Reduction with CCUS
Hydrogen-based direct reduced iron (H-DRI) combined with electric arc furnaces (EAF) is gaining traction, but residual emissions from natural gas reforming or incomplete capture necessitate CCUS. A 2024 OECD workshop highlighted breakthroughs in H2-DRI-CCUS hybrids, where captured CO2 from syngas production is stored or utilized in mineral carbonation. Projects like Sweden's HYBRIT initiative have scaled to industrial levels, reducing emissions by 95% when paired with CCUS, with costs falling to $600-800 per ton of steel produced. [OECD Steel Decarbonisation Workshop](https://www.oecd.org/en/events/2024/11/breakthrough-technologies-for-steel-decarbonisation.html)

## Innovations in Cement

### Electrified Kilns and Post-Combustion Capture
Cement production's process emissions (from limestone calcination) challenge electrification alone, making CCUS essential. In 2024, LEILAC 2 project in Belgium advanced indirect electrification of kilns, capturing 95% of CO2 from flue gases using calcium looping technology. This modular system avoids solvent degradation issues and integrates with oxyfuel combustion, achieving capture costs of €30-50 per ton. Complementary research from Nature Reviews Earth & Environment emphasizes clinker substitution alongside CCUS to cut emissions by 70-80%. [LEILAC Project Update](https://www.clepair.org/project/leilac-2); [Nature Review on Cement Decarbonization](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44296-025-00068-6)

### Utilization Pathways
Captured CO2 from cement plants is increasingly used in concrete carbonation, enhancing material strength while sequestering emissions. A DNV report forecasts that by 2030, CCUS capacity for cement could quadruple, driven by EU ETS incentives and falling costs (40% reduction by 2050). Innovations like those from CarbonCure inject CO2 into fresh concrete, mineralizing it permanently and reducing the sector's footprint by 5-10% per application. [DNV Energy Transition Outlook](https://www.dnv.com/energy-transition-outlook/)

## Policy and Investment Drivers

The U.S. DOE's 2024 Industrial Demonstration Program allocated $6 billion for CCUS projects in steel and cement, including hubs like the Heartland project capturing 1.5 MtCO2/year from Midwest facilities. Europe's Clean Industrial Deal (2024) provides €100 billion in funding, prioritizing CCUS deployment to meet 55% emissions cuts by 2030. Global investments reached $80 billion cumulatively by 2024, with steel and cement comprising 40% of CCUS pipelines. These initiatives address permitting bottlenecks, as noted in BloombergNEF's 2024 outlook, accelerating commercialization. [DOE Industrial Demonstration Program](https://www.energy.gov/articles/biden-harris-administration-announces-6-billion-industrial-demonstration-program-decarbonize); [BloombergNEF Industrial Decarbonization Watch](https://about.bnef.com/insights/industry-and-buildings/industrial-decarbonization-things-to-watch-2024/)

## Challenges and Future Outlook

Despite progress, challenges include high upfront costs ($500-1000 per ton CO2 captured) and infrastructure needs for CO2 transport. Integration with renewable energy sources is crucial to minimize lifecycle emissions. By 2030, CCUS could abate 15-20% of steel and cement emissions globally, scaling to 80% by 2050 with policy support. Ongoing pilots, such as those under the Global Cement and Concrete Association, underscore the need for international standards on embedded carbon measurement.
