# California Manufacturing Decarbonization Incentive Fund (MDIF) - April 2026

The Manufacturing Decarbonization Incentive Fund (MDIF) is a groundbreaking $4 billion initiative launched by the California Air Resources Board (CARB) to accelerate decarbonization in the state's manufacturing and refining sectors. Funded through California's Cap-and-Invest program, MDIF provides incentive allowances to covered entities for projects that deliver long-term greenhouse gas (GHG) emission reductions, compensating for the loss of federal incentives and supporting the transition to a clean economy.[https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/nc-MDIF%20FAQ_April%202026.pdf]

## Funding Mechanism

MDIF is financed by issuing up to 118 million new incentive allowances from a dedicated reserve account within the Cap-and-Invest system. These allowances are only released upon approval of eligible project applications and can be used for compliance obligations or sold on the market. Any unused allowances must be returned to CARB and permanently retired to ensure funds support actual decarbonization efforts. CARB will publicly report on fund distribution, including applications received, approvals, and remaining allowances, to maintain transparency and alignment with California's climate goals.[https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/nc-MDIF%20FAQ_April%202026.pdf]

## Eligibility Criteria

Eligible applicants include manufacturers and refiners subject to California's Cap-and-Invest Regulation, such as producers of cement, steel, food processing, and other industrial facilities receiving industrial allowance allocations. Projects must demonstrate quantifiable, long-term reductions in covered GHG emissions and meet specific regulatory criteria, including exemptions for biomass-derived fuels or methane mitigation that lowers emissions.[https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/nc-MDIF%20FAQ_April%202026.pdf]

## Supported Project Types

MDIF targets transformative investments in decarbonization technologies and processes. Eligible projects include:

- Replacement of fossil fuel-powered equipment with clean alternatives, such as electric or low-carbon systems.
- Adoption of low-carbon hydrogen that meets CARB's criteria for verifiable emission reductions.
- Onsite renewable energy generation (e.g., solar thermal, geothermal) that reduces or avoids fossil fuel combustion.
- Procurement of electrified thermal energy to displace onsite fossil fuel use.
- Use of alternative low-carbon materials to minimize process emissions, particularly in sectors like cement production.
- Carbon capture and sequestration initiatives, subject to approved quantification methodologies under section 95852.3 of the Cap-and-Invest Regulation.
- Methane emissions reduction projects that achieve covered GHG reductions.[https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/nc-MDIF%20FAQ_April%202026.pdf]

These projects aim to address hard-to-abate emissions in energy-intensive industries, fostering innovation and scalability.

## Application Process

Facilities submit electronic applications to CARB detailing the project scope, expected annual GHG reductions, fuel use changes, and eligibility justification. CARB staff review submissions for completeness and compliance, with approvals granted by the Executive Officer for a single compliance period. Key deadlines include:

- June 1, 2027, for the 2028 budget year.
- June 1, 2028, for 2029–2030 budget years.
- June 1, 2030, for 2031–2033 budget years.
- June 1, 2033, for 2034–2035 budget years.

The first applications are due by June 1, 2027, with allowances potentially awarded as early as late 2027 for use in 2028 compliance. Project expenditures can qualify starting September 1, 2026, pending board approval. CARB publishes complete applications quarterly (protecting confidential data) and updates on approvals regularly.[https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/nc-MDIF%20FAQ_April%202026.pdf]

## Reporting and Accountability

At the end of each compliance period, recipients must report on allowance usage, project implementation, and achieved GHG reductions. Failure to meet requirements results in the return of equivalent allowances for retirement. Incentive allowances are tracked via unique identifiers in the compliance registry, and CARB monitors market dynamics to prevent distortions. This ensures MDIF delivers verifiable progress toward California's net-zero ambitions.[https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/nc-MDIF%20FAQ_April%202026.pdf]

## Significance for Industrial Decarbonization

As part of a broader trend of states deploying capital for industrial transitions, MDIF positions California as a leader in bridging carbon pricing with direct investment in clean technologies. It supports sectors critical to global supply chains and complements federal efforts amid policy uncertainties.[https://nzero.com/blog/from-carbon-pricing-to-capital-deployment-states-are-creating-industrial-decarbonization-funds]
