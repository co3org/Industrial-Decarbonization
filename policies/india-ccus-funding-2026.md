# India CCUS Funding Initiative 2026

## Overview

In February 2026, the Indian government announced a significant allocation of Rs 20,000 crore (approximately $2.4 billion USD) over five years for carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies as part of the FY27 Union Budget. This initiative targets hard-to-abate sectors such as steel, cement, refining, and power, which face high emissions and substantial capital requirements for low-carbon transitions. The funding aims to accelerate industrial decarbonization, making clean technologies viable and supporting India's net-zero goals by 2070.

The move has bolstered confidence among domestic producers like steel and cement companies, signaling strong policy backing amid capacity expansion plans. It addresses the economic challenges of adopting CCUS, where costs can be prohibitive without government support. This allocation is part of broader efforts to enhance energy security and reduce reliance on fossil fuels in energy-intensive industries.

## Key Details

- **Budget Allocation**: Rs 20,000 crore over FY27–FY31, focused on CCUS deployment in high-emission industries.
- **Target Sectors**: Primarily steel and cement, which contribute significantly to India's industrial emissions (steel ~8% of national CO2, cement ~7%). Refining and power sectors also eligible.
- **Objectives**:
  - Subsidize CCUS retrofits and new facilities to capture and store CO2 from point sources.
  - Promote utilization pathways, such as CO2 in enhanced oil recovery or building materials.
  - Foster public-private partnerships to scale technologies and reduce abatement costs.
- **Implementation**: Managed through the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) and Department of Science & Technology, with incentives like tax credits and grants for pilot projects.
- **Expected Impact**: Potential to capture millions of tonnes of CO2 annually, aligning with India's updated NDCs and supporting global supply chain decarbonization.

## Challenges and Opportunities

India's industrial sector emits over 800 MtCO2e yearly, with steel and cement accounting for a large share due to process emissions and coal dependency. The funding addresses barriers like high capital costs (CCUS can add 20–50% to project expenses) and technology maturity. Opportunities include job creation in green tech, export of low-carbon products, and integration with renewable energy growth.

Companies like Tata Steel and UltraTech Cement have expressed interest in leveraging the funds for projects, potentially integrating CCUS with green hydrogen initiatives. This positions India as a leader in affordable CCUS for developing economies.

## Related Global Context

This initiative complements international efforts, such as the EU's Industrial Accelerator Act and US DOE funding, by focusing on cost-effective solutions for emerging markets. It also aligns with G20 commitments on CCUS cooperation.
