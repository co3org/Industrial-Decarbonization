# China's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030): Industrial Decarbonization Targets

*Published: March 2026*

China's 15th Five-Year Plan (FYP), adopted during the 2026 Two Sessions, outlines economic, social, and environmental priorities for 2026-2030. As the world's largest industrial emitter, China's plan emphasizes integrating decarbonization into industrial growth, focusing on energy efficiency, green technologies, and structural adjustments in heavy sectors. The plan shifts from energy consumption controls to a "dual-control" system for carbon emissions intensity and totals, targeting a 17% reduction in CO₂ emissions per unit of GDP by 2030.[^1]

## Key Industrial Decarbonization Goals

- **Energy Efficiency and Renovation**: Achieve energy savings of over 150 million tons of standard coal equivalent through renovations in key industries including thermal power, steel, non-ferrous metals, petrochemicals, chemicals, and building materials. Promote low-carbon technologies and establish green industrial zones with access to renewable energy resources.[^2]

- **Zero-Carbon Infrastructure**: Build approximately 100 national-level zero-carbon industrial parks and over 10,000 kilometers of zero-carbon transport corridors. These initiatives integrate clean electricity, green hydrogen supply, and advanced energy management to decarbonize industrial clusters and logistics.[^3]

- **Green Hydrogen and Fuels**: Expand green hydrogen applications in industry, transportation, and power. Launch pilots in five regions for hydrogen production, storage, and use in low-carbon steelmaking, industrial heating, and fuel production (e.g., green ammonia, methanol). Aim to reduce hydrogen end-use costs below 25 CNY/kg ($3.50/kg) and double the hydrogen fuel-cell vehicle fleet to 100,000.[^4]

- **Sector-Specific Measures**: Replace coal-fired boilers and kilns in industries like paper, food processing, and textiles with clean alternatives equivalent to 30 million tons of coal annually. Implement low-carbon renovations for coal chemical processing and enforce carbon emission assessments for all fixed-asset investment projects.[^5]

- **Carbon Control Mechanisms**: Introduce industry- and project-level carbon emission controls, including capacity management in high-emission sectors. By 2035, reduce economy-wide net GHG emissions by 7-10% from peak levels and build a climate-adaptive society.[^6]

## Funding and Policy Support

The National Low-Carbon Transition Fund will invest in frontier technologies like hydrogen, green fuels, and clean equipment. A four-year green hydrogen pilot program provides performance-based funding for commercialization. The plan supports R&D in fusion energy and advanced storage, positioning low-carbon industries as drivers of economic security and global leadership.[^7]

## Implications for Global Decarbonization

While the 17% carbon intensity target is less ambitious than the previous FYP's 18% (which China fell short of), the plan's focus on zero-carbon parks and hydrogen could accelerate deployment in hard-to-abate sectors. However, it lacks strict coal phase-out commitments, relying on renewables to offset fossil use. This approach may influence global supply chains, as China's industrial adjustments affect steel, chemicals, and metals markets. International collaboration, such as the 10-nation fusion alliance, could enhance technology transfer.[^8]

The plan embeds climate action within broader strategies for energy security and clean tech dominance, potentially overdelivering on renewables and hydrogen as seen in prior FYPs.
