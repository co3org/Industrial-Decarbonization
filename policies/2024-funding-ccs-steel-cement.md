# 2024 Government Funding Initiatives for CCS in Steel and Cement Decarbonization

Industrial sectors like steel and cement account for approximately 7-9% of global CO2 emissions, making their decarbonization critical for net-zero goals. Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) has emerged as a key technology to address process emissions in these hard-to-abate industries. In 2024, major government funding programs have accelerated CCS deployment, providing billions in support for demonstration projects and infrastructure.

## United States: $6 Billion Industrial Demonstration Program

The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) announced up to $6 billion in funding in March 2024 under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law to support innovative technologies for decarbonizing heavy industries, including steel, cement, and concrete. This Industrial Demonstration Program targets projects that capture at least 95% of CO2 emissions, with a focus on scaling solutions like CCS retrofits. For instance, one selected project at a cement plant aims to prevent 2 million tons of CO2 emissions annually through advanced capture systems. These initiatives are expected to create jobs, improve air quality, and drive replication across the sector, leveraging tax credits like 45Q to enhance economic viability.

## Germany: €6 Billion Climate Contracts for CCS

In 2024, Germany launched a €6 billion ($7 billion) program to decarbonize heavy industries via CCS, marking the first national climate contracts explicitly including this technology. The initiative supports hydrogen-based steel production, low-carbon cement, and CCS retrofits, blending policy certainty with performance-based incentives to attract private investment. Analysts project this could reduce emissions significantly by 2030, with costs for CCS expected to decline by 40% as technologies mature. Europe's emissions trading system further incentivizes adoption by aligning carbon prices with CCS economics.

## Global Context and Impacts

These programs align with international efforts, such as the IEA's tracking of new CCS facilities entering operation in 2023-2024, including projects in the U.S. and China that boost capture capacity. In steel, CCS complements hydrogen reduction, while in cement, it addresses calcination emissions, potentially cutting industry-wide CO2 by up to 36% when combined with other levers like clinker substitution. Challenges remain, including high capital costs and infrastructure needs, but these funding streams signal strong momentum toward scalable, near-zero emission processes.

## Key Takeaways

- **Investment Scale**: Over $13 billion committed in 2024 from the U.S. and Germany alone, focusing on CCS for steel and cement.
- **Technological Synergies**: CCS integrates with electrification and hydrogen to achieve >90% emissions reductions.
- **Policy Enablers**: Tax credits, emissions trading, and demonstration funding lower barriers for industry adoption.

This overview highlights actionable pathways for stakeholders to engage with these opportunities.
